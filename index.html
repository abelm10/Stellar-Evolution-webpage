1. Introduction: What is Stellar Evolution?

Start with the basics to grab your reader's attention.

Core Concept: Stellar evolution is the process by which a star changes over billions of years.

The Key Ingredients: Two main forces drive this process: gravity, which pulls inward, and nuclear fusion, which pushes outward.

The Deciding Factor: A star's initial mass is the most important factor in determining its entire life path and eventual death.

2. The Birth of a Star: From Nebula to Protostar üçº

Stellar Nurseries: Giant Molecular Clouds (GMCs) are vast, cold clouds of gas and dust.

Gravitational Collapse: A trigger, such as a nearby supernova shockwave, can cause a dense clump in the cloud to collapse under gravity.

The Protostar: As the clump collapses, it heats up and glows, forming a protostar. This is a baby star without nuclear fusion.

Ignition: When the core reaches around 15 million Kelvin, nuclear fusion begins, and a true star is born.

3. The Main Sequence: A Star's Adulthood ‚≠ê

Hydrostatic Equilibrium: A stable balance between gravity pulling inward and pressure from fusion pushing outward.

Hydrogen to Helium: The star fuses hydrogen into helium in its core.

Duration: A star spends about 90% of its life on the main sequence. Our Sun is in this phase.

4. The Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) Diagram

What it is: A chart that plots a star's luminosity (brightness) against its surface temperature (color).

Why it's important: It helps astronomers classify stars and track their evolution. The diagram shows the main sequence, giants, and dwarfs.
